# Evidence Reasoning and GPS-Aligned Conclusions

This is an original, compact operational summary written for this toolkit. It is
**not** a reproduction of any published standard, course, or manual. Where it
uses established terms (source, information, evidence, direct, indirect,
negative), those are ordinary genealogical vocabulary, not quotations.

Genealogy has no compiler: sources disagree, records are lost, and confidence is
probabilistic. The Genealogical Proof Standard (GPS) is the discipline the field
uses to decide *when a specific conclusion is well founded*. Read this file for
**how to reason**; the vault templates show **how to record** it.

A source, a fact, an assertion, or a research session cannot by itself be "GPS
compliant". Only a **written conclusion answering a named question** can be
evaluated. Do not describe genealogy as a hard science or imply laboratory-grade
certainty.

---

## 1. Treat every conclusion as an answer to a named question

Before judging anything as strong or weak, write the exact question it answers:
"Who were the parents of X, born ~1885 in Y?" — not just "X's parents".

A conclusion earns the strongest status only after its research coverage,
citations, analysis, conflict treatment, and written reasoning are adequate *for
that particular question*. No document type, evidence category, database count,
or confidence code can substitute for that evaluation.

---

## 2. GPS-aligned completion test

Address all five concerns before calling a conclusion `gps-supported`:

1. **Coverage** — explain why the sources you consulted are broad and deep
   enough for this question, and name meaningful sources you did **not** reach.
2. **Traceability** — give citations precise enough to relocate every item and
   every assertion you relied on (archive, fund/inventory/file, page/frame, URL,
   access date).
3. **Evaluation and correlation** — assess each assertion individually (§4–5),
   then compare assertions across records, people, places, and time.
4. **Conflict treatment** — identify and address material disagreements,
   including any you could not resolve.
5. **Written reasoning** — give an argument that connects the evidence to the
   *exact* conclusion, so a reader can follow and challenge it.

Completing one bounded research run does not by itself establish adequate
overall coverage. If coverage is unfinished, use a working status, not "proved".

---

## 3. Conclusion status is not an evidence level

Tag conclusions and atomic assertions with one of four **workflow** statuses.
These are local to this toolkit; they are not official GPS grades.

| Status | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `gps-supported` | The written conclusion addresses all five completion concerns for the exact question. |
| `provisional` | Best current answer, but coverage, correlation, or conflict resolution is incomplete. |
| `hypothesis` | A plausible proposition still being tested; alternatives remain live. |
| `unresolved` | Evidence is insufficient, materially conflicting, or not yet analyzed. |

Rules:
- Status belongs to a conclusion or an atomic assertion — **never** to a whole
  person or a whole document.
- `gps-supported` requires a written rationale and a coverage statement.
- Never assign status by counting sources.
- Never automatically downgrade oral information, derivative sources, secondary
  information, or indirect evidence — judge each on its own merits.

**Legacy note.** Older notes may use `Proven / Probable / Possible / Unproven`.
Read them, but write new notes with the four statuses above. Do not mechanically
translate an old label without reviewing the underlying assertion.

---

## 4. Analyze one assertion at a time

An **atomic assertion** is a single subject–predicate–object proposition, with
date/place qualifiers where needed: "P-0001 was born on 12 Mar 1885 in Y."

One document usually carries several assertions of very different quality. On a
death record, the death date may be firsthand and near-contemporaneous, while
the deceased's birth date and parents' names are secondhand and reported under
stress. Analyze each separately, recording:

- assertion ID and the exact assertion,
- the question it bears on,
- the document ID and pinpoint,
- what the item **literally says or shows**,
- your **interpretation** (kept separate from the literal reading),
- source form, informant knowledge, and evidence function (§5),
- extraction/transcription certainty,
- dependency group (§6),
- conflicts and live alternatives,
- conclusion status and rationale.

Keep four things visibly distinct: what the item says, what you transcribed,
what you inferred, and what you currently conclude.

---

## 5. Source, information, and evidence are separate assessments

Judge these three axes independently for **each assertion**. Do not collapse
them into one word, and do not treat any single value as a score.

**Source form** — the item the assertion comes from:
- *original record*, *derivative representation* (index, transcription,
  database, published book), *authored narrative*, or *unknown/uncertain*.
- Each form has different opportunities for error; evaluate in context. An
  original can still record a wrong fact; a careful transcription can be
  reliable. Do not treat "original" as automatically decisive, and never equate
  "original source" with "primary information".

**Information** — the origin of the statement:
- *firsthand*, *secondhand*, or *indeterminable*.
- Record the informant when known and whether the statement was made close to
  the event. Secondary information can still be correct; firsthand information
  can still be wrong.

**Evidence function** — how the assertion answers *this* question:
- *direct* (answers it outright), *indirect* (answers it only combined with
  other facts), or *negative* (a meaningful absence — §6).
- The same source can function as direct evidence for one question and indirect
  for another. Direct evidence can be inaccurate; a disciplined body of indirect
  evidence can support a strong conclusion.

---

## 6. Negative search results vs negative evidence

These are **different things**. Keep them in separate fields.

**Negative search result** — a bounded search found no matching entry. Record:
- repository and collection,
- exact person/date/place scope,
- the query and variants tried,
- index vs image coverage, and the pages/images actually inspected,
- access date and known limitations.

Most "not found" outcomes are only this: a scoped negative result. Report them
without drawing a broad absence conclusion.

**Negative evidence** — a documented absence that actually bears on a
conclusion. Use this label only when the note explains:
1. what entry or pattern was **expected**,
2. why record-keeping practice makes that expectation reasonable,
3. why the relevant records **survive** and were available,
4. why the searched range covers the expected time and place,
5. how spelling variants, indexing gaps, image gaps, and identity ambiguity were
   handled,
6. the exact assertion the absence is relevant to.

Always constrain the conclusion with a scope sentence: *"This absence bears only
on `<assertion>` within `<collection / place / date range / pages>`; it does not
establish absence from other collections, places, periods, or name variants."*

Never write "zero results eliminates X" unless that narrow elimination is
separately justified by the checklist above.

---

## 7. Provenance and independence

Two records agreeing is only corroboration if they are genuinely independent.
Track two dimensions of dependency:

- **Record provenance group** — items derived from the same underlying record or
  copy chain (an original and its index/transcription are **one** lineage, even
  on different websites).
- **Information-origin group** — assertions that trace to the same informant,
  narrative, or prior tree (two separately created records can still repeat one
  informant; church and civil copies may derive from one entry).

Rules:
- Different websites do not imply independence.
- Independence is assessed per assertion, not per document container.
- Independent agreement raises corroboration but never *automatically* proves a
  conclusion.
- Record known or suspected dependency in the document manifest.

---

## 8. Relationships and identity are assertions, not givens

Distinguish three levels, and never silently promote one to another:

1. **Explicitly stated** — the source names the relationship in words.
2. **Structural / role assertion** — a GEDCOM link, a household position, a
   witness or godparent role, or a shared surname. This reports someone's
   structure, not a proven fact.
3. **Inferred hypothesis** — your interpretation, to be tested.

Rules:
- Co-parenthood does not prove marriage.
- `HUSB`, `WIFE`, `FAMS`, `FAMC`, and `CHIL` report the GEDCOM submitter's
  structure. They do not by themselves establish marriage, sex/gender,
  biological parenthood, or legal status.
- Witnesses, godparents, neighbours, and household members are **associates**
  unless evidence supports kinship. Repeated association is a research lead, not
  proof of blood.
- Record relationship nature explicitly: `biological`, `adoptive`, `step`,
  `foster`, `guardian`, `social`, or `unspecified`. Never convert an unspecified
  link to biological parentage silently.
- Identity matching (is this the same person?) and relationship proof (how are
  they related?) are separate assertions.

---

## 9. FAN cluster / associates

When the direct line stalls, research the **F**riends, **A**ssociates, and
**N**eighbours — witnesses, godparents, in-laws, co-villagers, co-migrants,
unit-mates. Same-village families, marriage witnesses, and neighbours who moved
together often break a wall the direct record cannot. Treat every cluster tie as
a §8 association (a research lead) until evidence makes it a kinship assertion.

---

## 10. Conflict resolution

When assertions conflict (two birth years, two spellings, two villages), build a
matrix rather than picking a favourite:

- competing assertion,
- supporting document IDs,
- dependency groups (§7),
- information origin,
- context and the likely error mechanism,
- unresolved alternatives,
- rationale for acceptance or for continued uncertainty.

No "one better source" and no numeric majority resolves a conflict on its own.
If it cannot be resolved, leave the conclusion `provisional` or `unresolved` and
say why.

---

## 11. Written conclusion

A finished conclusion states:
- the exact question,
- the answer and its status,
- a coverage statement,
- the evidence chain,
- conflicts and how they were treated,
- limitations,
- citations,
- the next evidence that could change the conclusion.

---

## 12. The research loop

```
Question → Bounded run contract (research-run-protocol.md)
        → Search only the agreed scope (FAN cluster where planned)
        → Register each item in the document manifest
        → Analyze atomic assertions (source / information / evidence; dependency)
        → Separate negative results from negative evidence
        → Stop at the budget or a stop condition; list what was NOT searched
        → Review card → apply only authorized changes → next question
```

Always start from what is already known in the tree/vault. If there is no year,
place, or parent to anchor on, the first task is to find an anchor — not to
guess. The loop ends with a **review card**, not an immediate unreviewed vault
or GEDCOM change. See
[research-run-protocol.md](research-run-protocol.md).
