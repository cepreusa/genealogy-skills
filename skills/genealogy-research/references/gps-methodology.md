# The Scientific Method of Genealogy (GPS)

Genealogy has a formal, professional standard of proof — treat conclusions like
a scientist treats a hypothesis: state it, test it against independent evidence,
resolve conflicts, and write it down with citations. This file is the reference
for *how to reason*; the vault templates show *how to record* it.

---

## 1. The Genealogical Proof Standard (GPS)

The GPS (Board for Certification of Genealogists) defines when a conclusion is
"proved". A conclusion meets the standard only when **all five** are satisfied:

1. **Reasonably exhaustive research** — you searched the sources that could
   answer the question, not just the first one that gave a hit. Different record
   types, different repositories, and the FAN cluster (below).
2. **Complete and accurate source citations** — every fact traces to where it
   came from: archive, fund/opis/delo (fond/inventory/file), page/frame, URL,
   and the date you accessed it.
3. **Analysis and correlation** — you tested the quality of each piece of
   evidence (see §2) and compared independent sources against each other.
4. **Resolution of conflicts** — where sources disagree, you explain the
   discrepancy rather than silently picking one.
5. **A soundly reasoned, written conclusion** — the argument is stated in
   words, so a reader can follow and challenge it.

If any pillar is missing, the conclusion is not "proved" — downgrade it (see §4).

## 2. The Source → Information → Evidence model

(After Elizabeth Shown Mills, *Evidence Explained*.) Three independent axes —
do not collapse them into one word like "reliable".

**Source** — the physical/digital item:
- *Original* — the record itself (a parish register, an award sheet scan).
- *Derivative* — something copied from it (an index, a transcription, a
  database entry, a published book).
- Originals outrank derivatives; indexes contain transcription errors — verify
  against the scan whenever one exists.

**Information** — a statement within the source:
- *Primary* — recorded by someone with firsthand knowledge, at the time
  (birth date on a baptismal record written by the priest).
- *Secondary* — recorded later or by someone without direct knowledge (age at
  death reported by a grieving relative; a birthplace remembered decades later).
- A single document can hold both: on a death record the *death* date is
  primary, the *birth* date is secondary.

**Evidence** — how the information answers *your* question:
- *Direct* — answers it outright ("born 12 March 1901 in X").
- *Indirect* — answers it only in combination with other facts (an age of 43 in
  an 1944 document implies a birth year of ~1901).
- *Negative* — the meaningful *absence* of a record where one would be expected
  (see §5).

Best-quality evidence = **original source · primary information · direct
evidence**. Rank your findings on all three axes before trusting them.

---

## 3. FAN club / cluster research

Ancestors did not live in isolation. When the direct line hits a wall, research
the **F**riends, **A**ssociates, and **N**eighbours — witnesses, godparents,
in-laws, fellow villagers, co-migrants, military unit-mates. Identifying the
cluster around a person often breaks through a brick wall the direct record
cannot. Same-village families, marriage witnesses, and neighbours who moved
together are especially powerful.

---

## 4. Evidence tags used in this vault

Map GPS reasoning onto four working tags. Put one on every non-trivial claim:

| Tag | Meaning | Typical basis |
|---|---|---|
| **Proven** | Meets the GPS; conflicts resolved | Original + primary + direct, corroborated |
| **Probable** | Strong but not fully corroborated | Indirect evidence, matching time/place, one good source |
| **Possible** | A working hypothesis to test | Plausible lead, single weak/secondary source |
| **Unproven** | Insufficient evidence | Guess, family lore not yet checked |

Oral history from a direct descendant is a real source — tag it honestly
(often *Probable*), cite the person and date, and seek a document to confirm.

---

## 5. Negative evidence is a result

Knowing where a record is **not** is a genuine finding that saves months.
Whenever a search returns nothing, record it as data, not as a dead end:
- what you searched (person, years, place),
- where (archive/site, fund/collection),
- with what parameters,
- and what was *not* there.

A well-documented absence ("surname X has zero entries in parish Y, 1890–1910")
narrows the next step and prevents re-treading the same ground.

Distinguish two cases: (a) the record *should* exist here but doesn't → moves the
hypothesis; (b) the index simply doesn't cover these years → search originals
elsewhere. Note which one you concluded.

---

## 6. Correlation and conflict resolution

- Prefer **independent** sources — two derivatives of the same original are not
  two witnesses.
- When facts conflict (two birth years, two spellings, two villages), lay them
  side by side, weigh each on the Source/Information/Evidence axes, and write the
  reasoning for the one you accept — or leave it *Possible* if unresolved.
- A cluster of weak indirect clues pointing the same way can, together, reach
  *Probable* or even *Proven* — that is the essence of an **indirect-evidence
  proof argument**.

---

## 7. Classic traps that break proofs

These recur constantly; check them before trusting a match:

1. **Same-named places** — many villages share a name; a place may sit in a
   different district/uyezd/province than the modern one. *Verify the
   jurisdiction before searching its records.*
2. **Shifting administrative boundaries** — provinces and districts were
   redrawn; the "correct" region for 1900 may differ from today, which changes
   *which archive* holds the records.
3. **Surname spelling drift** — the same person appears spelled 3–5 ways across
   scribes, languages, and decades (endings, transliteration, Russification/
   Latinization). Search variants, not one fixed form.
4. **Same-named people** — a common name + right region is not identity; confirm
   with a second distinguishing fact (patronymic, spouse, exact date).
5. **Index vs. content** — many archive search engines index only *titles of
   files* (fond/opis/delo), not the names inside the registers. A surname
   "not found" there may simply mean the pages must be read by eye.
6. **Calendar differences** — Julian vs Gregorian (Russia used Julian until
   1918; add 12–13 days). Other communities may use religious calendars.
7. **Damaged / hard-to-read scans** — mold, water, fire; experts legitimately
   disagree on a word. Trust an indexer who held the original over a guess from
   a poor image.

---

## 8. The research loop

```
Question → Plan (what's known, what's missing, which sources)
        → Search (reasonably exhaustive; FAN cluster)
        → Analyze & correlate (Source/Information/Evidence; resolve conflicts)
        → Conclude with a tag (Proven/Probable/Possible/Unproven) + citation
        → Record in the vault (including negative results)
        → Next question
```

Always start from what is **already known** in the tree/vault before searching —
if there is no year, place, or parent to anchor on, the first task is to find an
anchor, not to guess.
