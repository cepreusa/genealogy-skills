## General Principles

1. **Spelling was not standardized** until the 19th–20th century. The same person may appear under 5+ spellings across documents
2. **Scribes wrote what they heard** — accent, dialect, and scribe's native language all affect spelling
3. **Always search variant spellings** — use wildcards when databases support them (e.g., `Kul*` to catch Kulesza, Kulesh, Kuleszanka)
4. **Women's surnames change** — maiden name, married name, and widow's form may all differ
5. **Indexes inherit scribe errors** — if the original says "Żobinca" instead of "z Sobisów," the index will too

## Slavic Languages

### Polish

| Form | Example | Usage |
|------|---------|-------|
| Male (base) | Kulesza | The man himself |
| Wife (-owa) | Kuleszowa | "wife of Kulesza" |
| Daughter (-anka/-ówna) | Kuleszanka | "daughter of Kulesza" |
| Adjective male (-ski) | Dembski | The man |
| Adjective female (-ska) | Dembska | Wife or daughter |
| "z" + genitive | z Dembskich | "née Dembska" (maiden name) |

**Common Polish surname suffixes and their meanings:**
- -ski/-ska: locative (from a place)
- -wicz/-ewicz: patronymic ("son of")
- -czyk/-czak: diminutive/occupational
- -ek/-ak: diminutive

### Russian

| Form | Example | Usage |
|------|---------|-------|
| Male | Кулеш / Кулеша | The man |
| Female (wife) | Кулеш / Кулешова | Wife (may add -ова/-ева) |
| Patronymic (male) | Юлианович | "son of Julian" |
| Patronymic (female) | Юлиановна | "daughter of Julian" |

**Russian suffixes added to non-Russian surnames:**
- -юк/-чук: "descendant of" (Drab → Драбюк)
- -ов/-ев: possessive (Кулеш → Кулешов)
- -ский: adjectival (Каминский)

### Ukrainian / Belarusian

Similar to Russian but with distinct suffixes:
- -енко: patronymic (Петренко = "son of Peter")
- -ук/-юк: patronymic (Кулешук)
- -ич: patronymic (Кулешевич)

## Germanic Languages

### German

| Form | Example | Usage |
|------|---------|-------|
| Base | Müller | Standard |
| Wife ("geborene") | Maria geb. Schmidt | "née Schmidt" |
| Umlaut variants | Mueller, Muller | Without special characters |
| Regional | Möller, Miller | Dialect variants |

**Common issues:**
- Umlaut dropping: ü→u, ö→o, ä→a (or ue, oe, ae)
- ß → ss or s
- Regional dialects: Schmied/Schmidt/Schmitt all = "smith"
- Prefix "von" may be dropped or added inconsistently

## Romance Languages

### French

- "dit" names: Pierre Boucher **dit** Belleville — alias used interchangeably with surname
- "de" particle: may be dropped in records or emigration
- Regional forms: Breton, Occitan, Alsatian variants of French names

### Italian

- "di" / "de" / "del" / "della": locative prefixes, sometimes dropped
- Feminine endings: -a (Maria Rossi → sometimes Maria Rossa in older records)
- Regional dialect spellings vary significantly (Sicilian vs. Tuscan vs. Venetian)

### Spanish / Portuguese

- Double surnames: paternal + maternal (García López)
- "de" particle: María de los Santos
- Portuguese: adds mother's maiden name FIRST, then father's surname

## Scandinavian Patronymics

Until 1800s–1900s, surnames were patronymic:

| Country | Pattern | Example |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Sweden | -son/-dotter | Erik Andersson = "Erik, son of Anders" |
| Norway | -sen/-datter | Erik Andersen |
| Denmark | -sen | same as Norway |
| Iceland | -son/-dóttir | still in use today |

**Farm names (Norway):** Often used as surnames — can change when a person moves to a different farm.

## Jewish Naming

### Ashkenazi

- **Secular name** (used in civil records) often differs from **Hebrew name** (used in synagogue records)
- **Patronymic**: Moshe ben Avraham ("Moses son of Abraham")
- **Forced surnames** (1787 Austria, 1804 Russia): often arbitrary — Goldberg, Rosenthal, Diamant
- **Matronymic** sometimes used: Rivkin ("of Rivka")

### Sephardic

- More stable surnames (often place-based: Toledo, Navarro)
- "ibn" patronymic in older records

## Latin in Church Records

Latin was used in Catholic records across Europe. Common patterns:

| Latin | Meaning | Example |
|-------|---------|---------|
| filius/filia | son/daughter | filius legitimus = legitimate son |
| uxor | wife | uxor eius = his wife |
| vidua | widow | vidua defuncti = widow of the deceased |
| honestus/honesta | respectable (social marker) | honesti Joannis = of respectable Jan |
| laboriosus | laborer / peasant | laboriosi Simonis = of laborer Szymon |
| de | from (place) | de Korzeniówka = from Korzeniówka |
| baptizatus est | was baptized | |
| natus est | was born | |
| obiit | died | |

**Latin name equivalents:**
| Latin | Polish | Russian | English | German |
|-------|--------|---------|---------|--------|
| Joannes | Jan | Иван | John | Johann |
| Franciscus | Franciszek | Франциск | Francis | Franz |
| Stanislaus | Stanisław | Станислав | Stanley | Stanislaus |
| Catharina | Katarzyna | Екатерина | Catherine | Katharina |
| Marianna | Marianna | Марианна | Mary Ann | Marianne |

## Transliteration and Russification

When Polish/Lithuanian territories came under Russian rule (post-1795 partitions), names were systematically transliterated:

| Polish | Russian transliteration | Notes |
|--------|------------------------|-------|
| Kulesza | Кулеша / Кулеш | Terminal -a often dropped |
| Dąbrowski | Домбровский | ą → ом |
| Dębski | Дембский | ę → ем |
| Łukasiuk | Лукасюк | Ł → Л |
| Szerszeń | Шершень | sz → ш |
| Kondraciuk | Кондрацюк | c → ц |

**Reverse transliteration is not always unambiguous** — multiple Polish spellings can produce the same Russian form.

## Common Traps

1. **Same person, multiple surnames**: A woman's maiden name, married name, and "z [maiden]" form may all appear as if they are different people
2. **Scribe errors perpetuated**: If a scribe misread "z Sobisów" as "Żobinca," the index preserves the error — search for both
3. **Patronymic ≠ surname**: In Russian records, "Юлианович" is a patronymic (father's name Julian), not a surname
4. **"z" confusion**: Polish "z" means "from/née" — "Marianna z Dembskich" means "Marianna née Dembska," not "Marianna from Dembski"
5. **Mother's maiden name in birth records**: Usually recorded as "z [surname in genitive]" — crucial for connecting families
6. **Spelling stabilizes over time**: Earlier records have wilder variation. The same family's surname may only stabilize in the 1880s–1900s
7. **Different scripts**: Same record might exist in Latin script AND Cyrillic script (church + civil copies) — search both
